Goals & DCA — the planning engine

A goal is a target dollar amount, an optional date, and a risk/strategy setting. From those few inputs Vantage works out the right account, the right securities, the target asset mix, and — once you add a contribution plan — whether you'll actually get there.

Create a goal like “Down Payment 2027, $20,000”. The engine reads four things — goal type, time horizon, risk, and strategy — and turns them into a concrete plan: which Wealthsimple account to fund it from (tax-aware), the top ETFs to buy, a cash/bond/equity glide target, and a live on-track / shortfall badge.

The four inputs that drive every recommendation

TYPEWHAT FOR

Withdrawal, DownPayment, Vacation, TaxBill, EmergencyFund, Income, Retirement, Education, Custom, or DayTrading. Sets the default behaviour.

HORIZONHOW SOON

Years to the target date. Short horizons automatically de-risk toward cash; long horizons allow more equity.

RISKSLIDER

VeryLow → Low → Moderate → High → Aggressive. Leave it on auto (horizon-derived) or set it explicitly to override.

STRATEGYPURPOSE

Income, Growth, Balanced, or Preservation. Optional overlay that biases the security mix without touching the tax logic.

How to use it

  1. Create the goalGoals tab → set a name, type, target amount, and (usually) a target date.
  2. Pick risk + strategy — or leave them autoAuto derives risk from the horizon. Set them explicitly to take control (see the override note below).
  3. Read the recommendationThe goal shows the best account with a one-line tax rationale, the top securities — each with a continuous fit score (real MER, yield, and discovery signal folded in, so near-identical ETFs still rank apart) and a one-line reason — and the glide-path bar.
  4. Link your real holdingsAttach existing positions at an allocation %, so progress reflects what you actually own.
  5. Add a DCA planA recurring contribution turns backward-looking progress into a forward projection (next section).

Risk & strategy: what each setting actually does

Risk tolerance sets the base palette of security categories and the target glide mix. On auto, a sub-1-year goal is VeryLow (cash), 1–3yr Low, 3–7yr Moderate, 7–15yr High, 15yr+ Aggressive. EmergencyFund is always cash-locked by definition.

Strategy is an optional overlay that sits on top: Income biases to vetted monthly payers, Growth drops dividend-flavoured picks and tilts to broad-equity + growth, Balanced guarantees a mix across dividend / growth / bond sleeves, and Preservation forces cash + short bonds regardless of type. Strategy never overrides the tax logic; that stays automatic.

Income uses risk as a hard product gate, not a cosmetic score boost. The minimum monthly yield is 2.5% for VeryLow, 3% for Low, 5% for Moderate, 6.5% for High, and 8% for Aggressive. Each product also has a minimum safe tier, so a leveraged credit fund cannot leak into a Low-risk goal just because its yield is high. Discovery uses this exact same contract. Goals and Discovery also use the same yield input: a TTM yield badge means provider metrics were available, while Est yield means the reviewed fallback was used. The fallback is never presented as live data.

Explicit risk beats horizon — with an honest warning

By default the horizon de-risks a near-dated goal to cash. But if you explicitly set a risk level, that conscious choice wins over the automatic de-risking — Vantage gives you the palette your risk + strategy imply rather than silently overruling you.

The trade-off is surfaced, not hidden: set (say) Aggressive on a goal due in under 3 years and the engine raises a risk-vs-horizon warning — “these holdings can swing 20–30%+ before your target date — that's the risk you're accepting with money you may need soon.” It only fires when your override is materially above what the horizon would pick (≥ 2 tiers up). EmergencyFund is the one exception — it stays cash-locked regardless.

The glide path

Every goal has a target cash/bond/equity split for its risk tier. As a goal nears its date (inside ~2 years) it automatically glides toward cash — unless you've set an explicit override. The goal page shows your actual equity weight versus this target and nudges you to add growth or trim risk.

Dollar-Cost Averaging — the patient game

Investing a fixed dollar amount on a schedule, no matter the price. In Vantage it's a contribution plan you attach to any buy-and-hold goal — and it's what lets a goal project forward instead of just tracking where you've been.

You set “$X every two weeks” and the app does the rest: it compounds your contributions at your goal's expected return, draws the path to your target date, and tells you whether you'll actually make it — or how much more per cheque you'd need.

The honest truth

Lump-sum investing beats DCA about two-thirds of the time historically — markets rise more often than they fall, so money in sooner usually wins. DCA's real edge isn't higher returns; it's discipline and lower regret: you keep buying through dips instead of trying (and failing) to time the bottom. For steady paycheque investing, that's exactly the point.

Where the return numbers come from

The projection isn't an invented rate. It blends the FP Canada / Institute of Financial Planning 2026 Projection Assumption Guidelines — the standard Canadian planners use — by your goal's cash/bond/equity mix, then subtracts a conservative 0.20% all-in ETF fee. Net nominal rates run from ~2.2% (all cash) to ~6.28% (all equity). The rate shown is the one the engine actually used.

How to use it

  1. Open or create a buy-and-hold goalGoals tab → e.g. your Down Payment 2027. Any goal that isn't Day Trading.
  2. Set the contribution planAdd an amount, a frequency (weekly / biweekly / monthly / quarterly), and a start date.
  3. Read the projectionThe goal now shows a projected value at your target date, an on-track / shortfall badge, and the dashed forward line on the glide chart.
  4. Act on the nudgeIf you're short, it tells you the exact amount to raise each contribution to. Bump it — or accept the later finish.
  5. Follow the splitEach contribution is carved across the ETFs the goal recommends, by weight — so you know exactly what to buy in Wealthsimple.

Day Trading — the prescriptive scanner

A DayTrading goal turns the page into a prescriptive trade-plan builder. It's no longer “here's a watchlist, go do your own analysis” — each candidate now carries a concrete plan: a suggested entry, a hard stop, a target, and a position size sized to risk ~1% of the goal's capital. Vantage still never places the order — you execute it yourself in Wealthsimple — but the homework is done for you.

Reality check — read this first

Each candidate has a suggested entry, a hard stop, a target, and a position size sized to risk ~1% of the goal's capital. Execute the plan and honor the stop — it's non-negotiable. This is still speculation, not investing: only ~1–4% of day traders are profitable long-term, so treat every plan as a hypothesis, not a guarantee. The tool gives you a disciplined structure; it can't give you an edge. One trade where you move the stop erases five clean wins.

Session-aware price, daily volatility — what's fresh and what isn't

The price is always the freshest real print we hold, labeled by the session it came from and stamped in ET: live (green dot) during regular hours, pre-market (04:00–09:30), after-hours (16:00–20:00), or close after the bell. It never shows an older daily close when a newer print exists, and never calls an hours-old print “live.” So overnight you'll see e.g. after-hours $6.32 · Tue 4:57 PM ET rather than the prior day's close. The trade-plan entry hangs off those current levels (break of today's high). Be honest about the rest: ATR% and relative volume are still end-of-day daily readings — and ATR% now also shows its dollar value (e.g. ATR 5.9% (≈$0.39)) so you see the literal range the stop and target are built from. Volatility is a daily measure, so a lagging EOD bar is expected, not a bug. A loud staleness banner fires only when the freshest price is itself more than a trading day old (market long closed, no recent print). One IEX caveat: the free Alpaca feed is a single venue, so extended-hours coverage is partial — thin names can have gaps before/after the bell, in which case the last regular print carries over (labeled “close”). Full consolidated extended-hours data needs the paid feed. The plan's levels are computed from those inputs — they are not predictions.

What every column means

PRICE / TODAYSESSION-AWARE

The freshest real print + today's % move (Alpaca, IEX), tagged by session — live (green dot, regular hours), pre-market, after-hours, or close — and stamped in ET. Never an older close when a newer print exists; never “live” off-hours.

ATR%WANT HIGH

Typical daily move, as a % of price — the size of the stop and target — shown with its $ value (e.g. 5.9% (≈$0.39)). 6% on a $40 stock ≈ $2.40 of range. End-of-day reading. Names under ~2% are filtered out.

RVOLWANT HIGH

Latest daily volume vs its 30-day average. >2× = unusual attention = it's actually in play. Also an end-of-day reading, not today's intraday tape.

$-VOLWANT HIGH

Liquidity. High = you get in and out instantly without slippage. Everything here clears a $5M/day floor.

CATALYSTTHE “WHY”

Earnings, an 8-K, news, an insider cluster — from the last 7 days. A move with a catalyst has follow-through; one without is noise.

AS OFFRESHNESS

The actual ET trade time of the displayed print (HH:MM ET, with the weekday when it's not today) — green when live. A pure prior-close fallback shows the bar date instead. A loud banner fires only when the freshest price is itself > 1 trading day old.

FITRANK

Composite 0–100 score for your chosen style. Higher = cleaner setup. Compare names within a style only — a MeanReversion 50 and a Momentum 85 are both strong for their style.

THE PLANTAP A ROW

Tap any row to expand its Entry / Hard stop / Target / Size — the prescriptive core. Every level is computed from ATR + recent bars + the 1%-risk rule.

Trade in your Personal / Margin account — gains are business income but losses are deductible. Not a TFSA (CRA reclassification risk — see Accounts & Tax). The goal's account card ranks Personal/Margin first and warns loudly if you have no non-registered account.

Five trading styles — each weights the signals and shapes the entry

Momentum — RVOL + catalyst + run · entry = break of today's high Breakout — near the highs + volume · entry = break of today's high ORB — opening-range: high ATR% + liquidity · entry = opening-range high MeanReversion — RSI extreme + room · entry = bounce near today's low Scalping — liquidity is everything · entry = current price, tight stop

Pick the style on the goal; the Fit score re-weights and the entry trigger changes with it. Trend styles (Momentum/Breakout/ORB) get a wider 1.5× ATR stop so noise doesn't shake them out; MeanReversion and Scalping get a tighter 1.0× ATR. Scalping is honestly flagged in its own reason line — most scalpers lose long-term.

What the Fit score means — and what it doesn't

Fit is a 0–100 per-style setup-quality score, not a rating of the company and not a direction call. For Momentum it's roughly 60% relative volume + a catalyst bonus + a little volatility — so the score answers “is this name in play for this style today,” nothing more.

That means a low score is a quiet/weak setup (relative volume near 1×, nothing moving), not a prediction the stock will drop. When the whole board is sub-50, that's the honest signal: no edge today — sit out. The strong days look different — the leaders print 70–80+ on 3–5× volume plus a fresh catalyst. Compare names within a style only; a MeanReversion 50 and a Momentum 85 are each strong for their own style.

The plan each candidate carries

Expand any row and you get four computed numbers, not a vibe:

ENTRYA LEVEL + TRIGGER

A real intraday level — the break of today's high (Momentum/Breakout), the opening-range high (ORB), a bounce near today's low (MeanReversion), or the current price (Scalping). It sits within ~1–4% of the live price, never a stale multi-day level while the market's open.

STOPHARD · ATR-BASED

entry − 1.0–1.5×ATR, shown with the $ and % distance. This is the line you do not move.

TARGET+2R

entry + 2×risk — a fixed 2:1 reward:risk. You can be right under half the time and still net positive.

SIZE1% RISK

From the 1%-risk rule: risk 1% of the goal's trading capital, shares = floor(1%-capital ÷ stop-distance). Shows the position $ and the actual $-at-risk after rounding.

The 0-share guardrail: a pricey, volatile name can come back as 0 shares — that means one share's stop risk already exceeds your 1% budget. That's the rule protecting you, not a glitch: skip the name, or widen your risk deliberately and knowingly.

How long do you hold? — exit timing

The plan tells you where to get out; this tells you when. There's no magic “ideal” hold time — you're out the moment the trade is done, wrong, stalled, or the day is ending, whichever comes first. Four exit triggers, in priority order:

  1. Target or stop — that's most of your exitsThese are your primary exits and they're already on the plan. Target hit → take the win. Stop hit → out, no debate, no “let it come back.” The vast majority of trades end right here; the rest of this list is for the trade that does neither.
  2. Time-stop — bail on a stalled tradeIf a momentum / breakout / ORB setup isn't following through ~30–60 min after entry — just chopping sideways, going nowhere — get out even though it never hit the stop. A momentum trade that isn't moving is a failed setup; don't tie up capital and risk waiting for a move that isn't coming.
  3. Flat by the close — alwaysIf neither stop nor target has triggered by ~3:50 PM ET, close it anyway. No exceptions. This is the line that keeps a day trade a day trade.
No overnight holds — this is the defining rule

Being flat by the bell, every single day, is what makes this day trading rather than swing trading. Hold overnight and two things happen: the trade quietly becomes a swing trade you never planned or sized for, and the stock can gap straight through your stop on overnight news you can't react to — the order doesn't fill at your stop, it fills wherever the open lands. Gapping through a stop on a position that's suddenly too big is one of the top ways day traders blow up an account. Out by the close — every time.

Rough windows by style: a scalp lasts seconds to minutes. A momentum / breakout / ORB trade usually resolves in the first 1–2 hours — if it hasn't moved by then it's probably dead money, so the time-stop applies. And nothing is held past the close. Shorter is better: intraday edges decay fast, so the longer you sit in a trade that isn't working, the less reason there is to still be in it.

The fine print the plan already handles

The 1% rule is baked in: on a $25k day-trade goal, the size is computed so the stop risks ~$250 (1%) — you don't do the math, the plan does. If a single share's stop already exceeds that, the size is 0 by design.

Currency caveat: your capital is CAD; a US name's levels are USD. Shares are sized USD-risk against the CAD budget 1:1 (a close approximation) — convert for an exact position. The row discloses this.

Wealthsimple is your secret weapon: commission-free means a small target actually survives. At a $10/trade broker, small day trades are dead on arrival.

Tax: in your Personal account, frequent-trading gains are business income (100% inclusion at your marginal rate) — gross is well above net. This is a grind, not a lottery.

Discovery — ideas by purpose and risk

The full engine scores the market at 18:00 ET on weekdays, with cached re-ranks at 10:30 and 13:30. It starts with fundamentals such as earnings growth, revenue, margins, valuation, profitability, and balance-sheet health, then adds news, insider buying, earnings, filings, and momentum. The page re-ranks that evidence around what you are actually looking for.

Each ticker gets a single composite score (roughly 0–10 for healthy names) built from 14 weighted signals. The score is fully transparent: every component is stored, so you (or the chat) can see exactly why a name ranked where it did — even the unflattering parts.

Purpose lenses and risk

Growth rewards revenue, EPS, margins, and momentum. Income requires a vetted monthly payer and weighs yield quality, payout, profitability, and balance-sheet support. Catalyst prioritizes recent earnings beats, insider clusters, material filings, analyst upgrades, and follow-through. Quality/value emphasizes durable profitability, margins, valuation, and financial strength. Raw leaves the saved 14-signal composite untouched.

The risk selector changes what is allowed, not just the sorting. For Income, the minimum yields are 2.5% / 3% / 5% / 6.5% / 8% from VeryLow through Aggressive, and every row names its product-risk floor. Live provider yield wins when it is usable; a labeled curated estimate fills genuine data gaps. Quarterly payers do not pass the Income lens.

The scoring funnel

Compare & Watchlist — is the new thing better than what I own?

Compare scores your holdings against the market using the same discovery engine, then tells you — in plain English — whether any candidate actually beats what you already hold. Watchlist is the lighter “keep an eye on this” list that feeds it.

Compare — Holdings vs Market

One unified table ranks your held positions and the top candidates side by side, score descending, with held rows tinted and an ownership dot. Each row carries a verdict — a deterministic, no-LLM action label — and the Top Swaps panel surfaces the best held-vs-candidate pair where a swap is genuinely worth considering. The raw 0–10 scores are normalized for rotation math: the candidate must clear 6.0 and lead the holding by at least 3.0 points, with a Weakening or Broken thesis required for an automatic rotation.

The Compare workflow

  1. Scan the unified rankingWhere do your holdings sit relative to the best candidates the engine found?
  2. Read the Top Swaps panelIt only proposes a swap when the candidate clearly out-scores a holding by a meaningful margin.
  3. Check the verdict + rationaleEach row's one-liner tells you the “so what.” A BUY needs confirming signals, not just a high number.
  4. Refresh to recomputeDiscovery runs nightly at 6pm ET; the Refresh button triggers a fresh compute on demand.

Watchlist

Tickers you're tracking but don't own. Each entry shows recent catalyst coverage (articles in the last 14 days) and the latest headline, so you can see whether a name is heating up. Add or remove entries from the page, or just tell the chat “add RKLB to my watchlist.” Watchlisted names remain visible in Discovery with a status badge, and they also feed the monthly allocation and discovery digests.

Accounts & Tax — the right wrapper for every holding

Vantage models your Wealthsimple sub-accounts (TFSA, RRSP, Spousal RRSP, RESP, LIRA, RRIF, Personal, Margin, Corporate) with their currencies and contribution room — then applies Canadian tax law to recommend where each holding belongs and to flag mistakes.

The big idea: where you hold something matters almost as much as what you hold. A US dividend ETF in a TFSA quietly leaks 15% withholding tax forever; the same ETF in an RRSP doesn't, thanks to the Canada-US treaty. Vantage bakes these rules in so you place holdings correctly without being a tax expert.

Wealthsimple FX still matters

Stock and ETF commissions are zero, but currency conversion is not automatically free. A funded USD account avoids conversion on each US trade; converting between CAD and USD can still incur Wealthsimple's FX fee. For a Canadian company or equivalent exposure, Vantage therefore prefers a suitable CAD-listed security when it avoids unnecessary FX without changing the investment thesis.

The tax-placement matrix

The rules it encodes

US DIVIDENDSRRSP

The Canada-US treaty exempts the 15% withholding on US dividends held in an RRSP/RRIF/LIRA. In a TFSA or RESP it's unrecoverable.

CDN DIVIDENDSTFSA / NON-REG

Eligible Canadian dividends get the dividend tax credit in non-registered, and compound tax-free in a TFSA. The credit is lost inside an RRSP.

GROWTHTFSA

Capital gains on big winners are tax-free forever in a TFSA — the single best wrapper. Non-reg is workable (only 50% of gains taxed).

INTERESTRRSP

Bonds, HISA ETFs, and high-yield interest are taxed as ordinary income — shelter them in a registered account, not non-reg.

Day trading is the exception that inverts everything

For a DayTrading goal the logic flips: registered accounts are the wrong home. Frequent trading in a TFSA can be reclassified by the CRA as carrying on a business, making your “tax-free” gains fully taxable (Canadian Western Trust (Ahamed) v. The King, 2023 TCC 17). An RRSP is statutorily exempt from that reclassification, but withdrawals are taxed as ordinary income and permanently destroy room — still the wrong home. The engine ranks Personal / Margin first, and if you have no non-registered account it raises a hard warning. (Corporate accounts are treated as a valid non-registered home but get a caveat — corporate investment-income tax differs.)

How to use it

  1. Add your sub-accountsAccounts tab → record each account's type, currency, and contribution room.
  2. Tag positions to accountsEach holding knows which account it lives in, so placement advice is concrete.
  3. Read the per-goal account pickEvery goal recommends a best account with a one-line tax rationale, and warns about RRSP-only setups for spend-soon goals.
  4. Watch the conflict flagsIf two goals overflow a single account's room, the goals page surfaces it.

Alerts & Digests — the always-on monitor

Behind the dashboard, a worker continuously ingests news, filings, earnings, insider trades, analyst moves, prices, and macro data — then turns meaningful events into alerts and packages routine updates into scheduled digests, delivered over Telegram. Advisory only: it never trades.

The ingestion → alert pipeline

Event types the engine watches

Earnings Earnings beat 8-K / material 8-K Breaking news Intraday move Sector news Macro Sentiment spike Insider cluster Analyst upgrade

The catalyst engine

A step beyond single alerts: hourly during market hours, the catalyst engine groups recent events per ticker and measures conjunction — is this just one signal, two corroborating signals, or a full triplet (insider + earnings + 8-K)? Stronger conjunction earns a buy suggestion from Claude (sized, cited, cap-checked), tagged so you can see why it fired. A full triplet is rare enough to bypass the cooldown.

The digest schedule

What each digest is for

Morning (7:00, weekdays): overnight news on your holdings, pre-market movers, earnings today, catalysts in the next 5 days. Flags thesis-status changes; no buy suggestions.

Evening (16:30, weekdays): the day's winners/losers, after-hours earnings that just reported, tomorrow's calendar, any thesis pillars that weakened today.

Weekly deep-dive (Sun 20:00): a more thoughtful synthesis across your whole book — shared macro exposure, a diversification audit, and a review of theses gone stale (>30 days unvalidated).

Monthly allocation (1st, 09:00): takes your monthly budget and proposes specific buys — held names below target weight, watchlist names with strong catalysts, and the most-mentioned unheld tickers — each cap-validated so two buys can't blow the budget.

Discovery digest (Sat 10:00): surveys the market for names you're not looking at yet, with buy suggestions on unheld names and rotation ideas out of weakening positions.

Where alerts live + the Ops view

Every alert and suggestion is also an Insight on the dashboard, with a chronological feed, filter chips, and Bought / Pass actions. The Calendar shows scheduled catalysts (earnings, 8-Ks, macro) for the next 14 days. The Ops page is your observability cockpit: last 50 job runs, today's vs month-to-date LLM spend, per-source health, and a recent-errors feed.

Portfolio Tools — hold, rebalance, validate, prove

The tools that manage the money you already have: a multi-account, multi-currency holdings view, rebalance suggestions when you drift past your caps, a thesis engine that keeps checking why you own each name, and a backtester to pressure-test a strategy before you trust it.

Portfolio — your holdings, properly

The main holdings view tracks positions across all your sub-accounts in both CAD and USD, with live prices (Alpaca → Finnhub → yfinance, with a daily-bar fallback), per-position P&L, a currency toggle, an account filter, and a sector donut for concentration at a glance. Add positions one at a time or bulk-import from a pasted CSV (preview → confirm). Your shares and cost basis are treated as brokerage truth — read-only, never edited by the engine or chat.

Rebalance — stay inside your own rules

You set a single-position cap and a sector cap in Settings. The rebalance engine checks your concentration against them and, when something drifts, asks Claude for a specific trim / rotate / exit (or a fresh buy if you have cash) — every suggestion cited, cap-validated, cooldown-checked, and de-duplicated so you don't get the same idea twice in 24 hours. Trims that reduce an over-concentration are allowed even when a naive cap check would block them.

Theses — keep knowing why you own it

The Theses page is the at-a-glance health view across all positions; per-position detail (pillars, risks, evaluation history) lives on each holding's page. Compare uses thesis health directly: a Broken thesis becomes an EXIT verdict, a Strengthening one with room becomes ADD.

Backtest — prove it on history first

A deterministic, reproducible harness that replays a strategy over historical prices — no LLM in the loop, so the same inputs always give the same result. Pick a strategy (monthly-allocation, rebalance-only, or catalyst-driven), a date range, and starting capital; get summary metrics, an equity curve versus a benchmark, and the full trade log. It respects the same single-position and sector caps as the live engine, so what you test resembles what you'd actually run.

  1. Keep holdings + accounts currentAccurate positions and account tags are what make every other tool's advice concrete.
  2. Write a thesis per positionEven a short one — it's what lets the engine tell you when the story changes.
  3. Set your caps in SettingsSingle-position and sector caps drive both rebalance suggestions and Compare verdicts.
  4. Backtest before you commit to a ruleConfirm a strategy would have behaved sanely before you let the live engine suggest it.
